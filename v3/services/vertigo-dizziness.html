Understanding Vertigo and Dizziness

Vertigo and dizziness are among the most common reasons people visit a healthcare provider, yet these symptoms are frequently misdiagnosed or inadequately treated. Many patients suffer for weeks, months, or even years before receiving an accurate diagnosis and effective treatment. The good news is that the majority of vertigo and dizziness conditions respond exceptionally well to physical therapy, often with rapid and dramatic improvement.

At Resolve Physical Therapy in Bend, Oregon, we provide specialized vestibular assessment and treatment for patients experiencing vertigo, dizziness, balance problems, and related symptoms. Jenny McAteer, DPT, has advanced training in vestibular rehabilitation and uses a systematic evaluation process to identify the specific cause of your symptoms and design a targeted treatment plan. Many patients with BPPV, the most common cause of vertigo, experience complete resolution of symptoms in just one to three treatment sessions.

Vertigo vs. Dizziness: What Is the Difference?

While the terms vertigo and dizziness are often used interchangeably, they describe different sensations that provide important diagnostic clues. Understanding the distinction helps your physical therapist determine the underlying cause and select the most effective treatment approach.

Vertigo is a specific type of dizziness characterized by a false sensation of rotational movement. Patients with vertigo feel as though they or the room around them is spinning, tilting, or swaying. This spinning sensation is typically triggered or worsened by head position changes, such as rolling over in bed, looking up, or bending forward. Vertigo almost always indicates a problem within the vestibular system, either in the inner ear (peripheral vertigo) or in the brain (central vertigo).

Dizziness is a broader term that can include vertigo but also encompasses several other sensations. Lightheadedness is a feeling of faintness or near-fainting that is often related to blood pressure changes, dehydration, or cardiovascular issues. Disequilibrium is a sense of imbalance or unsteadiness, particularly when standing or walking, that may result from vestibular, neurological, or musculoskeletal problems. Presyncope is the sensation that you are about to pass out and is typically related to cardiovascular or autonomic nervous system dysfunction.

BPPV: The Most Common Cause of Vertigo

Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo, commonly known as BPPV, is the single most common cause of vertigo and accounts for approximately 50 percent of all peripheral vestibular disorders. BPPV occurs when tiny calcium carbonate crystals called otoconia become dislodged from the utricle (a gravity-sensing organ in the inner ear) and migrate into one of the three semicircular canals. The semicircular canals detect rotational head movements, and when the displaced crystals shift within these canals, they send false signals to the brain indicating that the head is moving when it is not. This creates the characteristic brief but intense spinning sensation that patients experience with position changes.

Symptoms of BPPV

BPPV produces episodes of intense rotational vertigo that are triggered by specific head position changes. Common triggers include rolling over in bed, lying down or sitting up, looking up (such as reaching for a high shelf), and bending forward (such as tying your shoes). Each episode of spinning typically lasts less than one minute, though the residual nausea and unsteadiness can persist for longer. Between episodes, many patients feel relatively normal, though some experience lingering motion sensitivity and mild unsteadiness throughout the day.

The Epley Maneuver and Canalith Repositioning

The primary treatment for BPPV is canalith repositioning, a series of carefully guided head and body movements that move the displaced crystals out of the affected semicircular canal and back to the utricle where they belong. The Epley maneuver is the most well-known repositioning technique and is used for the posterior canal, which is the most commonly affected canal. Other repositioning techniques, including the Semont maneuver, the BBQ roll, and the Appiani maneuver, are used for horizontal canal and anterior canal variants of BPPV.

At Resolve Physical Therapy, your therapist will first perform the Dix-Hallpike test and other positional assessments to confirm the diagnosis and determine which ear and which canal are affected. This step is critical because the repositioning maneuver must be performed on the correct side and for the correct canal to be effective. Once the affected canal is identified, your therapist will guide you through the appropriate repositioning maneuver during the same visit. Most patients experience significant or complete relief within one to three treatment sessions.

Other Causes of Vertigo

While BPPV is the most common cause of vertigo, several other conditions can produce similar symptoms. Accurate diagnosis is essential because each condition requires a different treatment approach.

Vestibular Neuritis

Vestibular neuritis is an inflammation of the vestibular nerve, typically caused by a viral infection. It produces sudden, severe vertigo that lasts for several days to weeks, often accompanied by nausea, vomiting, and difficulty with balance. Unlike BPPV, the vertigo in vestibular neuritis is constant rather than episodic and is not triggered specifically by position changes. Treatment includes vestibular rehabilitation exercises designed to promote central compensation, the brain's process of adapting to the altered vestibular input. Gaze stabilization exercises, balance training, and habituation exercises are the cornerstones of treatment for vestibular neuritis.

Meniere's Disease

Meniere's disease is a chronic inner ear condition characterized by episodes of vertigo lasting 20 minutes to several hours, fluctuating hearing loss (typically low-frequency), tinnitus (ringing in the ear), and a sensation of fullness or pressure in the affected ear. The condition is thought to result from abnormal fluid dynamics within the inner ear. While medical management is the primary treatment for Meniere's disease, vestibular rehabilitation can help manage the balance problems and motion sensitivity that develop between acute episodes. Your physical therapist at Resolve PT will design an exercise program to improve your balance confidence and reduce your sensitivity to movement during remission periods.

Labyrinthitis

Labyrinthitis is an infection or inflammation of both the vestibular nerve and the cochlea (hearing organ) in the inner ear. Like vestibular neuritis, it causes sudden severe vertigo, but labyrinthitis also produces hearing loss and tinnitus because the cochlea is involved. Treatment follows a similar vestibular rehabilitation approach to vestibular neuritis, with the addition of medical management for the hearing component. Early initiation of vestibular rehabilitation has been shown to accelerate recovery and reduce the duration of symptoms.

Types of Dizziness Beyond Vertigo

Not all dizziness involves the spinning sensation of vertigo. Understanding the specific quality of your dizziness helps your physical therapist narrow down the potential causes and design the most effective treatment plan.

Lightheadedness

Lightheadedness is a sensation of near-fainting or feeling woozy. It is often related to blood pressure changes, particularly when standing from a sitting or lying position (orthostatic hypotension). Dehydration, medication side effects, anxiety, and cardiovascular conditions can also cause lightheadedness. While lightheadedness is not typically a vestibular problem, your physical therapist at Resolve PT can perform screening tests to determine whether the cause is positional blood pressure changes, cervicogenic (neck-related) dizziness, or vestibular dysfunction, and refer you to the appropriate provider if medical evaluation is needed.

Disequilibrium

Disequilibrium is a sense of unsteadiness or imbalance, particularly when standing or walking. It often feels as though the ground is uneven or moving beneath your feet. Disequilibrium can result from vestibular dysfunction, peripheral neuropathy, musculoskeletal weakness, visual impairment, or neurological conditions. Physical therapy for disequilibrium focuses on progressive balance training, strengthening of the lower extremities, sensory integration exercises, and fall prevention strategies. For older adults in the Bend community, disequilibrium is a significant risk factor for falls, making early evaluation and treatment important for maintaining independence and safety.

Presyncope

Presyncope is the feeling that you are about to lose consciousness. It is typically caused by a temporary reduction in blood flow to the brain, which can result from cardiac arrhythmias, orthostatic hypotension, vasovagal responses, or dehydration. While presyncope is primarily a medical condition rather than a vestibular problem, your physical therapist can screen for these causes and coordinate referral to the appropriate physician if needed. If orthostatic hypotension is identified, your therapist can teach you strategies for managing position changes safely.

Vestibular Assessment at Resolve PT

Accurate diagnosis is the foundation of effective vertigo and dizziness treatment. At Resolve Physical Therapy, your initial evaluation includes a comprehensive assessment designed to identify the specific cause of your symptoms and guide treatment planning.

Dix-Hallpike Test

The Dix-Hallpike test is the gold standard for diagnosing posterior canal BPPV. During this test, your therapist will guide you from a seated position to lying down with your head turned to one side and slightly extended. If displaced crystals are present in the posterior canal, this position will trigger a characteristic pattern of eye movement (nystagmus) and vertigo symptoms. The direction and timing of the nystagmus tells your therapist exactly which ear and which canal are affected, allowing precise treatment with the appropriate repositioning maneuver.

Head Thrust Test (Head Impulse Test)

The head thrust test evaluates the function of the vestibulo-ocular reflex (VOR), which is the eye movement response that keeps your vision stable during head movements. Your therapist will ask you to focus on a target while quickly turning your head to each side. If the vestibular system on one side is underperforming, your eyes will be unable to maintain fixation on the target during the head turn and will make a corrective eye movement (catch-up saccade) after the head stops. This test helps identify unilateral vestibular hypofunction from conditions like vestibular neuritis.

Balance Testing

Your therapist will assess your standing and dynamic balance under multiple conditions, including eyes open and closed, on firm and compliant surfaces, and with head movements. This systematic approach reveals how well your visual, vestibular, and somatosensory systems are contributing to balance control. Deficits in any of these systems can contribute to unsteadiness and fall risk. Standardized balance assessments such as the Berg Balance Scale, the Dynamic Gait Index, and the Sensory Organization Test provide objective baselines that are used to track improvement throughout treatment.

Oculomotor Examination

Your therapist will evaluate your eye movements including smooth pursuit (tracking a moving target), saccades (rapid eye movements between targets), and gaze stability during head movements. Abnormal eye movement patterns provide important information about the location and nature of vestibular dysfunction and help differentiate between peripheral and central causes of vertigo.

Treatment Approaches

Treatment for vertigo and dizziness is tailored to the specific diagnosis identified during your evaluation. At Resolve Physical Therapy, we use evidence-based treatment techniques that have been proven effective through clinical research.

Canalith Repositioning

For BPPV, canalith repositioning maneuvers are the first-line treatment and are effective in over 90 percent of cases. Your therapist will select the appropriate maneuver based on which semicircular canal is affected. The procedure is performed in the clinic and takes approximately 10 to 15 minutes. You may be asked to avoid certain positions for a brief period after treatment and may need one to two follow-up visits to confirm resolution.

Habituation Exercises

Habituation exercises involve repeated exposure to specific movements or positions that provoke dizziness symptoms. With consistent repetition, the brain learns to suppress the inappropriate vestibular signal, and the dizziness response gradually diminishes. This approach is particularly effective for patients who experience motion sensitivity or dizziness with visual stimuli such as scrolling screens, busy visual environments, or head movements during daily activities.

Gaze Stabilization Exercises

Gaze stabilization exercises train the vestibulo-ocular reflex to maintain clear vision during head movements. These exercises involve focusing on a stationary target while moving your head at progressively faster speeds and in multiple directions. Over time, the brain adapts and the VOR becomes more accurate, reducing the blurring and visual instability that many vestibular patients experience with head movement. Your therapist will provide a home exercise program with specific speed, duration, and frequency targets to guide your progression.

Balance Training

Progressive balance training addresses the unsteadiness and fall risk that accompany many vestibular conditions. Exercises are designed to challenge your balance through progressive reduction of support (from two legs to one leg, from firm surfaces to foam), visual challenges (eyes open to eyes closed), and dynamic tasks (standing to walking, turning, reaching). For older adults in the Bend community who are at risk for falls, balance training is a critical component of vestibular rehabilitation that can significantly reduce fall risk and improve confidence in daily activities.

When to Seek Emergency Care

While most causes of vertigo and dizziness are benign and treatable with physical therapy, certain warning signs require immediate emergency medical evaluation. Seek emergency care if your vertigo or dizziness is accompanied by any of the following:

  • Sudden severe headache that is different from any headache you have experienced before
  • Numbness, weakness, or tingling in the face, arm, or leg, especially on one side of the body
  • Difficulty speaking, slurred speech, or trouble understanding others
  • Sudden vision loss, double vision, or visual field cuts
  • Difficulty walking, loss of coordination, or inability to stand
  • Chest pain, rapid heart rate, or shortness of breath
  • Loss of consciousness or fainting

These symptoms can indicate a stroke, transient ischemic attack, or other serious neurological or cardiovascular condition that requires immediate medical intervention. If you are experiencing vertigo or dizziness without these warning signs, scheduling an evaluation at Resolve Physical Therapy is an appropriate and effective first step toward diagnosis and treatment.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between vertigo and dizziness?

Vertigo is a specific type of dizziness characterized by a false sensation of spinning or rotational movement. It feels as though you or the room around you is rotating. Dizziness is a broader term that can include vertigo but also encompasses lightheadedness, unsteadiness, disequilibrium, and a floating sensation. The distinction matters because vertigo typically points to a vestibular (inner ear or brain) cause, while other forms of dizziness may have cardiovascular, neurological, or musculoskeletal origins. A proper evaluation at Resolve PT can help identify the specific type and cause of your symptoms.

How quickly can BPPV be treated?

BPPV is one of the most treatable causes of vertigo. The Epley maneuver and other canalith repositioning techniques can resolve symptoms in one to three treatment sessions for most patients. Many patients experience significant relief after a single session. At Resolve PT, your physical therapist will perform the Dix-Hallpike test to confirm the diagnosis and determine which ear canal is affected, then perform the appropriate repositioning maneuver during your first visit.

Can a physical therapist treat vertigo without a doctor's referral?

Yes. Oregon is a direct-access state, which means you can see a physical therapist for vertigo and dizziness evaluation without a physician referral. If your physical therapist at Resolve PT identifies symptoms that require medical evaluation or imaging, we will coordinate a referral to the appropriate specialist. For most cases of BPPV and vestibular dysfunction, physical therapy is the first-line treatment and can begin immediately.

When should I go to the emergency room for vertigo?

You should seek emergency medical care if your vertigo or dizziness is accompanied by sudden severe headache, numbness or weakness in the face, arms, or legs, difficulty speaking or understanding speech, vision loss or double vision, difficulty walking or loss of coordination, chest pain, or loss of consciousness. These symptoms can indicate a stroke or other serious neurological condition that requires immediate medical attention. If your symptoms are limited to positional vertigo without these warning signs, scheduling an evaluation at Resolve PT is the appropriate first step.
