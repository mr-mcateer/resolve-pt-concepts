Check Your Balance

Simple tests you can do at home and what your results mean

  1. Home

Medically reviewed by Jenny McAteer, DPT Published June 25, 2020 · Last reviewed March 2026
June 25, 2020 · 9 min read

Balance is one of the most fundamental yet underappreciated aspects of physical health. Most people do not think about their balance until something goes wrong—a stumble on uneven ground, a near-fall getting out of the shower, or a bout of dizziness that makes the room seem to spin. But balance is not a fixed trait. It can be measured, trained, and improved at any age. And because balance declines gradually, testing it regularly can help you catch problems early, before a fall causes a serious injury.

At Resolve Physical Therapy in Bend, Oregon, balance assessment is a core component of our vestibular and fall-prevention programs. This guide provides simple tests you can perform at home, explains what your results might mean, and describes when it is time to seek professional help.

How Balance Works

Balance is not controlled by a single system. It is the result of three sensory systems working together in real time:

  • Vision. Your eyes provide information about your position relative to the environment. This is why balance is more challenging with your eyes closed or in the dark.
  • Vestibular system. The inner ear contains specialized structures (the semicircular canals and otolith organs) that detect head movement, rotation, and position relative to gravity. The vestibular system is your internal gyroscope.
  • Somatosensory system (proprioception). Receptors in your muscles, joints, and the soles of your feet provide information about body position, joint angles, and the surface you are standing on. This system tells your brain where your body is in space without you needing to look.

Your brain integrates information from all three systems and generates the muscle responses needed to keep you upright. If one system is impaired—due to aging, injury, disease, or deconditioning—the other systems must compensate. When two or more systems are compromised simultaneously, fall risk increases dramatically.

Simple Balance Tests to Try at Home

Safety first: Perform all balance tests near a sturdy surface like a countertop, table, or wall that you can grab if you lose your balance. Have someone nearby to assist if needed. Stop immediately if you feel dizzy, lightheaded, or unsteady.

Test 1: Single-Leg Stance (Eyes Open)

Stand on one leg with your arms at your sides. Lift the other foot a few inches off the floor without hooking it behind your standing leg. Start a timer and measure how long you can hold the position before touching the raised foot down, grabbing a support, or wobbling significantly. Test both sides.

What the results mean:

  • Under 60 years old: You should be able to hold a single-leg stance for at least 30 seconds with minimal wobbling.
  • 60 to 69 years old: A hold of at least 22 seconds is considered normal.
  • 70 to 79 years old: At least 14 seconds is the expected benchmark.
  • 80 years and older: At least 5 seconds. If you cannot maintain a single-leg stance for 5 seconds at any age, your fall risk is elevated and you should seek a professional balance assessment.

Test 2: Single-Leg Stance (Eyes Closed)

Repeat the single-leg stance test with your eyes closed. This eliminates the visual system from the balance equation and places greater demand on your vestibular and proprioceptive systems. Most people find this significantly harder than the eyes-open version.

What the results mean: Closing your eyes typically reduces balance time by 50% or more. If your balance time drops dramatically (more than 75%) with eyes closed, it may indicate that you are overly reliant on vision for balance and your vestibular or proprioceptive systems need strengthening.

Test 3: Tandem Walk (Heel-to-Toe)

Walk in a straight line placing the heel of one foot directly in front of the toes of the other foot, as if walking on a tightrope. Take 10 steps forward, looking straight ahead rather than at your feet. Arms can be at your sides or extended for balance.

What the results mean: You should be able to complete 10 steps without stepping to the side, reaching for a wall, or losing the heel-to-toe pattern. Difficulty with tandem walking suggests vestibular dysfunction, proprioceptive deficits, or general deconditioning. This test is commonly used in clinical settings as a quick screening for fall risk.

Test 4: Timed Up and Go (TUG)

Sit in a standard chair with armrests. On the word "go," stand up from the chair, walk 10 feet at a comfortable pace, turn around, walk back, and sit down again. Time yourself from the moment you start to stand until you are fully seated again.

What the results mean:

  • Under 12 seconds: Normal mobility and low fall risk.
  • 12 to 20 seconds: Moderate mobility. Some risk of falls, especially in challenging environments.
  • Over 20 seconds: Significant mobility limitation and elevated fall risk. A professional assessment is recommended.

Test 5: Functional Reach Test

Stand next to a wall with your arm extended at shoulder height, parallel to the floor. Without moving your feet, reach forward as far as you can along the wall. Measure the distance between your starting position and your maximum reach. This test measures your anterior stability limit—how far you can lean forward without losing your balance.

What the results mean: A functional reach of less than 10 inches is associated with an increased risk of falls. A reach of less than 6 inches indicates a very high fall risk and warrants immediate professional evaluation.

What Causes Poor Balance?

If your test results suggest room for improvement, there are many possible contributing factors. Some are easily addressed, while others require professional evaluation.

  • Normal aging. All three balance systems decline gradually with age. The vestibular system loses approximately 40% of its sensory cells by age 70, and proprioceptive acuity decreases due to changes in joint receptors and reduced nerve conduction speed.
  • Vestibular disorders. Conditions like benign paroxysmal positional vertigo (BPPV), vestibular neuritis, and Meniere's disease directly affect the inner ear balance system and can cause dizziness, vertigo, and significant balance problems.
  • Peripheral neuropathy. Damage to the nerves in the feet (common in diabetes) reduces proprioceptive input and makes it harder to detect changes in the surface beneath your feet.
  • Muscle weakness. Weakness in the ankles, hips, and core reduces the body's ability to generate the corrective movements needed to recover from a loss of balance.
  • Medications. Many common medications, including blood pressure drugs, sedatives, antidepressants, and anti-anxiety medications, can cause dizziness or drowsiness that impairs balance.
  • Vision changes. Bifocal or progressive lenses can distort depth perception when looking at the ground, and conditions like cataracts or macular degeneration reduce the visual input available for balance.
  • Inactivity. Balance is a skill that improves with practice and declines with disuse. People who become less active due to pain, illness, or lifestyle changes often experience a gradual decline in balance that accelerates over time.

Exercises to Improve Your Balance

The good news is that balance responds well to training at any age. Research consistently shows that balance exercises reduce fall risk in older adults by 23% to 40%. Here are four exercises you can start today:

Single-Leg Stance Practice

Start by standing on one leg near a countertop for safety. Hold for 30 seconds on each side, twice daily. As your balance improves, progress to eyes closed, standing on a pillow, or adding head turns while balancing.

Heel-to-Toe Walking

Practice the tandem walk for 20 steps in each direction, two to three times daily. Keep your gaze forward, not down at your feet. Progress by walking tandem on a slightly raised surface like a line of tape on the floor.

Weight Shifts

Stand with feet hip-width apart and slowly shift your weight from one foot to the other, pausing at each end for three to five seconds. Progress to lifting the unweighted foot slightly off the floor at each end of the shift.

Standing on Varied Surfaces

Practice standing on different surfaces: a folded towel, a foam balance pad, a pillow, or grass. Each surface challenges your proprioceptive system differently and builds adaptability.

When to See a Physical Therapist

You should see a physical therapist for a comprehensive balance assessment if any of the following apply:

  • You have fallen in the past year or have had a near-fall.
  • You feel unsteady when walking, especially on uneven surfaces or stairs.
  • You experience dizziness, lightheadedness, or a spinning sensation.
  • Your balance test results are below the benchmarks described above.
  • You have a medical condition that affects balance (diabetes, Parkinson's, stroke history, vestibular disorder).
  • You are afraid of falling and have started limiting your activities as a result.
