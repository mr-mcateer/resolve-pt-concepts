Check Your Posture

A simple guide to better alignment from your physical therapist

Medically reviewed by Jenny McAteer, DPT Published July 8, 2020 · Last reviewed March 2026
July 8, 2020 · 9 min read

Posture is one of those things most of us think about only when someone reminds us to sit up straight. But the way you hold your body throughout the day has a real impact on how you feel, how you move, and how well you recover from physical activity. Poor alignment can contribute to neck pain, headaches, shoulder dysfunction, low back pain, and even reduced breathing capacity. The good news is that with awareness and a few targeted exercises, most postural issues can be improved significantly.

At Resolve Physical Therapy in Bend, Oregon, postural assessment is part of nearly every evaluation we perform. Understanding how your body is aligned at rest tells us a great deal about which muscles are overworked, which are underactive, and where movement restrictions may be contributing to pain. This guide will help you perform a basic posture check at home and give you practical strategies for improvement.

The Wall Test: A Quick Posture Assessment

Stand with your back against a flat wall. Place your heels about two to four inches from the baseboard. Your buttocks, upper back, and the back of your head should all touch the wall without effort. Now check the following:

  • Head position: Can the back of your head touch the wall comfortably, or does it feel like you are straining to push it back? If your head does not naturally rest against the wall, you likely have forward head posture.
  • Lower back gap: Slide your hand between your lower back and the wall. Ideally, you should be able to fit your flat hand in the space. If the gap is significantly larger (a fist or more), you may have excessive lumbar lordosis. If there is almost no gap, you may have a flattened lumbar curve.
  • Shoulder blade contact: Both shoulder blades should rest against the wall without rounding forward. If your shoulders are visibly forward of the wall, you have rounded shoulder posture.

Common Postural Patterns

Forward Head Posture

Forward head posture is the most prevalent postural deviation in our modern society. It is driven by hours spent looking at computer screens, phones, and tablets. When the head migrates forward of the shoulders, the cervical extensors (muscles on the back of the neck) must work overtime to hold the head up, while the deep neck flexors on the front become weak and elongated.

For every inch the head moves forward of neutral, the effective weight the neck muscles must support increases by approximately 10 pounds. This increased mechanical load is a major contributor to tension headaches, neck stiffness, and pain between the shoulder blades.

Rounded Shoulders

Rounded shoulders often accompany forward head posture. The pectoral muscles on the chest become shortened and tight, pulling the shoulders forward, while the rhomboids and middle trapezius on the upper back become stretched and weak. This pattern reduces the space available for the rotator cuff tendons and can contribute to shoulder impingement over time.

Excessive Lumbar Lordosis

An exaggerated inward curve of the lower back, sometimes called "swayback" or anterior pelvic tilt, often results from tight hip flexors combined with weak abdominal and gluteal muscles. This pattern is common in people who sit for extended periods, as prolonged sitting keeps the hip flexors in a shortened position. Over time, the tilted pelvis increases compressive forces on the lumbar facet joints and can contribute to low back pain.

Flat Back Posture

The opposite of excessive lordosis, flat back posture involves a loss of the natural lumbar curve combined with a posterior pelvic tilt. This pattern places more stress on the intervertebral discs and is associated with hamstring tightness and weak hip flexors. People with flat back posture often report difficulty standing for extended periods and may lean forward slightly when walking.

Exercises to Improve Your Posture

The following exercises target the most common postural imbalances. Perform them daily for best results. Each exercise takes less than two minutes, so the entire routine can be completed in under 15 minutes.

Chin Tucks

Sit or stand tall. Without tilting your head, gently draw your chin straight back as if making a double chin. Hold for five seconds, then relax. This exercise strengthens the deep neck flexors and counteracts forward head posture. Perform 10 to 15 repetitions, several times throughout the day.

Doorway Pectoral Stretch

Stand in a doorway with one arm raised to 90 degrees at the elbow. Place your forearm against the door frame and gently step through the doorway until you feel a comfortable stretch across the front of your chest and shoulder. Hold for 30 seconds on each side. Repeat with your arm at a higher position to target different portions of the pectoral muscle.

Thoracic Extension Over a Foam Roller

Lie on your back with a foam roller positioned across your mid-back, just below your shoulder blades. Support your head with your hands and gently extend backward over the roller. You should feel a comfortable stretch in your upper back without pain. Hold for five seconds and repeat five to eight times, moving the roller to different segments of the thoracic spine.

Band Pull-Aparts

Hold a resistance band at shoulder height with both hands, arms extended in front of you. Pull the band apart by squeezing your shoulder blades together, keeping your arms straight. Slowly return to the start. This exercise strengthens the rhomboids and middle trapezius, which are typically weak in people with rounded shoulders. Perform two to three sets of 15 repetitions.

Prone Y-T-W Raises

Lie face down on the floor or a bench with your arms hanging down. Raise both arms into a Y position (overhead at 45 degrees), hold for three seconds, then lower. Repeat in a T position (straight out to the sides) and then a W position (elbows bent, squeezing the shoulder blades). This series targets the lower trapezius, rotator cuff, and posterior shoulder muscles. Perform two sets of eight repetitions of each letter.

Hip Flexor Stretch (Half-Kneeling)

Kneel on one knee with the other foot in front. Tuck your pelvis slightly by squeezing the glute on the kneeling side and shift your weight forward until you feel a stretch in the front of your hip. Hold for 30 seconds per side. This stretch addresses the hip flexor tightness that contributes to excessive lumbar lordosis and anterior pelvic tilt.

Ergonomic Tips for Your Workspace

Exercise alone will not fix your posture if your work environment constantly pulls you back into poor alignment. Here are practical adjustments you can make today:

  • Monitor height: Position your screen so the top of the display is at or slightly below eye level. This prevents the downward head tilt that drives forward head posture.
  • Chair height: Adjust your chair so your feet are flat on the floor and your thighs are parallel to the ground. Your hips should be at or slightly higher than your knees.
  • Keyboard and mouse placement: Keep your elbows close to your body at approximately 90 degrees. Your wrists should be in a neutral (straight) position, not angled upward or downward.
  • Take movement breaks: Stand up and move for at least two minutes every 30 to 60 minutes. Set a timer if needed. Brief movement breaks prevent the sustained loading patterns that reinforce poor posture.
  • Phone habits: Hold your phone at eye level instead of looking down. Extended phone use in the "text neck" position is one of the fastest ways to develop forward head posture.

When Posture Problems Need Professional Help

While many postural issues respond well to self-correction exercises, some require professional evaluation. See a physical therapist if your postural problems are accompanied by persistent pain that does not improve with exercise, numbness or tingling in your arms or legs, headaches that worsen with sitting or computer work, or if you have a structural condition like scoliosis that affects your alignment.
