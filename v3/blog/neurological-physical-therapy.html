Understanding Neurological Physical Therapy

How targeted rehabilitation helps the brain and body reconnect

  1. Home
  2. Blog

Medically reviewed by Jenny McAteer, DPT Published May 6, 2021 · Last reviewed March 2026
May 6, 2021 · 9 min read

Neurological physical therapy is a specialized area of rehabilitation focused on helping people who have experienced damage or disease affecting the brain, spinal cord, or peripheral nervous system. Unlike general orthopedic physical therapy, which primarily addresses musculoskeletal injuries, neurological PT targets the communication pathways between the brain and the body to restore movement, improve balance, and rebuild functional independence.

At Resolve Physical Therapy in Bend, Oregon, we work with patients managing a wide range of neurological conditions, from Parkinson's disease and stroke recovery to multiple sclerosis and traumatic brain injury. This article explores how neuro PT works, why it is effective, and what you can expect from treatment.

What Is Neuroplasticity and Why Does It Matter?

Neuroplasticity is the brain's ability to reorganize itself by forming new neural connections throughout life. For decades, scientists believed that the adult brain was essentially fixed—that once neurons were damaged, the lost function was permanent. Research over the past 30 years has fundamentally changed that understanding. We now know that the brain can adapt, rewire, and recruit new areas to take over functions that were previously handled by damaged regions.

This discovery is the foundation of modern neurological physical therapy. When a patient suffers a stroke, for example, the area of the brain that controlled certain movements may be damaged. Through intensive, repetitive, task-specific practice, physical therapy can help neighboring brain regions learn to perform those same functions. The key principles that drive neuroplastic change include:

  • Repetition and intensity. The brain needs hundreds or thousands of repetitions of a movement to form strong new neural pathways. Higher-intensity practice yields faster results.
  • Specificity. The training must closely match the real-world task the patient wants to improve. Practicing reaching for a cup will improve cup-reaching ability more than generic arm exercises.
  • Salience. The brain changes more readily when the task is meaningful and important to the patient. Motivation matters.
  • Timing. While neuroplasticity exists throughout life, the window of most rapid recovery after a neurological event is typically the first three to six months. Early intervention matters, though improvement can continue for years.

Conditions Treated with Neurological PT

Parkinson's Disease

Parkinson's disease is a progressive neurological condition that affects the dopamine-producing neurons in a region of the brain called the substantia nigra. As these neurons deteriorate, patients experience tremor, rigidity, slowness of movement (bradykinesia), and postural instability. Physical therapy for Parkinson's focuses on maintaining and improving mobility, balance, and functional independence as the disease progresses.

One of the most evidence-based approaches for Parkinson's patients is LSVT BIG, a program that uses high-amplitude movements to counteract the tendency toward smaller, slower movement patterns. Research has shown that LSVT BIG can improve walking speed, stride length, balance, and the ability to perform daily tasks. Patients typically attend four sessions per week for four weeks, with daily home practice that reinforces the movement patterns learned in the clinic.

Other effective interventions for Parkinson's include dual-task training (practicing a cognitive task while walking, for instance), boxing-inspired exercise programs, and treadmill training with visual or auditory cues to improve gait rhythm.

Stroke Recovery

Stroke is the leading cause of long-term disability in the United States, and physical therapy is a cornerstone of post-stroke rehabilitation. The type and severity of deficits depend on which area of the brain was affected and how much tissue was damaged. Common impairments include weakness or paralysis on one side of the body (hemiparesis or hemiplegia), difficulty with balance and coordination, spasticity, and reduced sensation.

Neurological PT for stroke recovery typically begins in the acute care hospital and continues through inpatient rehabilitation and outpatient therapy. Treatment may include constraint-induced movement therapy (CIMT), which restricts the unaffected arm to force use of the impaired arm, task-specific training for activities like dressing and cooking, balance retraining on varied surfaces, and gait training with or without assistive devices.

Multiple Sclerosis

Multiple sclerosis (MS) is an autoimmune condition in which the immune system attacks the myelin sheath that insulates nerve fibers in the brain and spinal cord. This disrupts the transmission of nerve signals and can cause fatigue, weakness, spasticity, balance problems, numbness, and cognitive changes. Because MS follows an unpredictable course with periods of relapse and remission, physical therapy must be adaptive and responsive to the patient's current status.

Exercise is one of the most important interventions for people with MS. Contrary to earlier beliefs that exercise might worsen symptoms, research has consistently shown that regular physical activity improves strength, endurance, balance, and quality of life in MS patients. A physical therapist can design an individualized exercise program that accounts for heat sensitivity (a common MS trigger), fatigue management, and the patient's current functional level.

Traumatic Brain Injury

Traumatic brain injuries range from mild concussions to severe injuries that cause prolonged loss of consciousness. Physical therapy for TBI addresses the physical consequences of the injury, including balance deficits, dizziness, coordination problems, and difficulty with complex movements. For patients with vestibular symptoms following a concussion, vestibular rehabilitation can reduce dizziness and improve balance significantly.

What to Expect in Neurological PT

Your first visit at Resolve Physical Therapy will include a comprehensive evaluation of your strength, balance, coordination, range of motion, sensation, and functional abilities. Your physical therapist will assess how you move through everyday tasks like standing up from a chair, walking, turning, and reaching. Based on this assessment, they will develop a treatment plan with specific, measurable goals tied to the activities that matter most to you.

Treatment sessions typically last 45 to 60 minutes and involve a combination of:

  • Functional task practice (standing, walking, reaching, stepping)
  • Strengthening exercises targeting weakened muscle groups
  • Balance training on stable and unstable surfaces
  • Gait training with cueing strategies
  • Stretching and mobility work for spasticity management
  • Home exercise program instruction

Progress is measured through standardized outcome measures that track changes in balance, walking speed, functional independence, and fall risk over time. These objective measurements help guide treatment decisions and demonstrate real improvement.

The Importance of Early and Ongoing Intervention

One of the most important messages in neurological rehabilitation is that earlier intervention generally leads to better outcomes. After a stroke, the first three months represent a critical window when the brain is most receptive to change. For progressive conditions like Parkinson's disease, starting physical therapy early—even before significant disability develops—can help maintain function and slow decline.

Equally important is the concept of ongoing care. Neurological conditions often require periodic reassessment and adjustment of the exercise program as the condition evolves. Patients may benefit from intensive blocks of therapy interspersed with periods of independent home exercise, returning to the clinic when new challenges arise or when they need guidance progressing their program.

If you or a family member is living with a neurological condition, physical therapy can make a meaningful difference in daily function and quality of life.
