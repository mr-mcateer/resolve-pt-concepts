The Role of Imaging in Physical Therapy

When imaging helps, when it does not, and what the results actually mean

  1. Home
  2. Blog
  3. Imaging in Physical Therapy
Medically reviewed by Jenny McAteer, DPT Published June 26, 2020 · Last reviewed March 2026
June 26, 2020 · 9 min read

One of the most common questions patients ask when they come to physical therapy is whether they need an X-ray or MRI first. It is a reasonable question. Many people assume that a scan is a necessary starting point before any treatment can begin. However, the relationship between imaging and physical therapy is more nuanced than most people realize, and in many cases, imaging is not only unnecessary but can sometimes lead to confusion, unnecessary procedures, and delays in getting effective treatment.

This article explains when imaging is genuinely helpful, when it can be safely deferred, and how physical therapists at Resolve Physical Therapy in Bend, Oregon use imaging findings to inform—but not dictate—treatment decisions.

Types of Diagnostic Imaging

Understanding the different imaging modalities and what each one shows helps put scan results in proper context.

X-Rays (Radiographs)

X-rays produce images of dense structures, primarily bones. They are the fastest, most affordable, and most widely available form of imaging. X-rays are excellent for identifying fractures, joint space narrowing (a sign of arthritis), bone spurs, and skeletal alignment. However, X-rays do not show soft tissues like muscles, tendons, ligaments, or cartilage. If your injury involves a ligament tear, tendon strain, or disc problem, an X-ray will not detect it.

MRI (Magnetic Resonance Imaging)

MRI uses powerful magnets and radio waves to create detailed images of soft tissues, including muscles, tendons, ligaments, cartilage, nerves, and intervertebral discs. MRI is the gold standard for evaluating soft tissue injuries and provides far more detail than X-rays. However, MRI is expensive, time-consuming (typically 30 to 60 minutes in the scanner), and not always covered by insurance without prior authorization.

CT Scans

CT scans use X-ray technology to create cross-sectional images of the body. They provide better bone detail than standard X-rays and can also visualize some soft tissue structures. CT scans are most commonly used in physical therapy settings for complex fractures, spinal stenosis assessment, and post-surgical evaluation.

Ultrasound Imaging

Diagnostic ultrasound uses sound waves to create real-time images of soft tissues. It is increasingly used in physical therapy for evaluating tendon injuries, muscle tears, and joint effusions. Ultrasound is portable, relatively inexpensive, and does not involve radiation. Some physical therapy clinics use ultrasound imaging during treatment to visualize muscle activation and guide needle-based interventions like dry needling.

When Imaging Is Needed

There are clear clinical situations where imaging is important and should not be delayed:

  • Suspected fracture: If you have experienced a traumatic injury (fall, direct blow, high-speed accident) and have localized bony tenderness, swelling, and inability to bear weight or use the limb, an X-ray is appropriate to rule out or confirm a fracture.
  • Suspected serious pathology: Red flag symptoms such as unexplained weight loss, history of cancer, fever with spine pain, severe and progressive neurological deficits (such as loss of bowel or bladder control), or symptoms that do not improve with rest may indicate conditions that require immediate imaging and referral.
  • Pre-surgical planning: If surgery is being considered, imaging is essential for the surgeon to understand the anatomy and plan the procedure. MRI is typically required before arthroscopic shoulder or knee surgery, and X-rays or CT scans are needed for joint replacement planning.
  • Failure to progress: If a patient has completed a reasonable course of physical therapy (typically six to eight weeks) without meaningful improvement, imaging can help identify structural problems that may require a different treatment approach.

When Imaging May Not Be Necessary

Here is where things get interesting. Research over the past two decades has consistently shown that many findings on imaging studies are present in people who have no pain or symptoms at all. This means that what appears on a scan does not always explain why you hurt.

Consider the following research findings:

  • Lumbar spine: Studies of people with no back pain have found that 30% of 20-year-olds and up to 84% of 80-year-olds have disc degeneration visible on MRI. Disc bulges are present in about 40% of asymptomatic adults. These findings are a normal part of aging, much like gray hair or wrinkles.
  • Shoulder: Research has shown that partial rotator cuff tears are present on MRI in up to 22% of people over 60 who have no shoulder pain. Full-thickness tears have been found in up to 28% of people over 60 without symptoms.
  • Knee: Meniscus tears are present on MRI in approximately 35% of people over 50 with no knee pain.

These statistics do not mean that imaging is useless. They mean that imaging findings must be interpreted in the context of the clinical picture—your symptoms, your physical examination, your history, and your functional limitations. A disc bulge on MRI is only clinically meaningful if it correlates with your specific pain pattern and neurological signs.

The Risk of Premature Imaging

Getting imaging too early in the treatment process can sometimes do more harm than good. When patients receive MRI results showing disc bulges, degenerative changes, or partial tears, they often become fearful of movement and convinced that they have a serious structural problem that requires surgical intervention. This fear and catastrophizing can actually worsen pain and delay recovery.

Research has also shown that early MRI for low back pain (within the first six weeks, without red flag symptoms) is associated with higher rates of surgery, higher healthcare costs, and no better outcomes compared to patients who received physical therapy first. Multiple clinical guidelines, including those from the American College of Physicians, recommend against routine imaging for nonspecific low back pain in the first four to six weeks.

How Physical Therapists Use Imaging

When imaging has been performed, physical therapists use the results as one piece of the diagnostic puzzle. We never treat an MRI—we treat the person. Imaging helps us understand the structural landscape, but the clinical examination tells us which structures are actually causing symptoms and how best to address them.

For example, if a patient comes in with an MRI showing a disc bulge at L4-L5, our evaluation will determine whether that disc bulge is the source of their pain (by testing nerve tension, sensation, reflexes, and specific movement patterns) or whether the pain is coming from a different source entirely, such as a facet joint, sacroiliac joint, or muscular dysfunction.

Physical therapists are also trained to identify when imaging is needed and when it is not. In Oregon, physical therapists have direct access, meaning you can be evaluated and begin treatment without a physician referral. If during our evaluation we identify signs that imaging is warranted, we will refer you to the appropriate provider for scanning and follow up on the results.

The Bottom Line

Imaging is a valuable tool when used appropriately. It is essential for ruling out fractures, identifying serious pathology, and planning surgical interventions. But for many common musculoskeletal complaints—low back pain, neck pain, shoulder pain, and knee pain—a thorough physical examination is often more informative than a scan, and treatment can begin immediately without waiting for imaging results.
