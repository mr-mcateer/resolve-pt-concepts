Simple Ways to Improve Your Heart Health

Evidence-based strategies for a stronger cardiovascular system

February is American Heart Month, a time to focus on cardiovascular health and the role that daily habits play in protecting the heart. Heart disease remains the leading cause of death in the United States, but the encouraging news is that many of the risk factors are modifiable. Regular physical activity, in particular, is one of the most powerful tools available for reducing cardiovascular risk, and physical therapists are uniquely trained to help people exercise safely and effectively.

At Resolve Physical Therapy in Bend, Oregon, we see firsthand how movement transforms health. Whether you are recovering from a cardiac event, managing a chronic condition, or simply trying to build a healthier lifestyle, the strategies below can help you take meaningful steps toward a stronger heart.

How Exercise Benefits Your Heart

The heart is a muscle, and like any muscle, it responds to training. Regular aerobic exercise strengthens the heart's pumping capacity, improves the efficiency of oxygen delivery to working tissues, and helps the cardiovascular system recover more quickly from exertion. Beyond these direct effects, consistent physical activity also addresses several major risk factors for heart disease:

  • Blood pressure. Regular aerobic exercise can reduce systolic blood pressure by 5 to 8 mmHg on average, an effect comparable to many first-line blood pressure medications.
  • Cholesterol. Exercise raises HDL (the protective form of cholesterol) and can lower LDL and triglycerides when combined with dietary changes.
  • Blood sugar. Physical activity improves insulin sensitivity and helps regulate blood glucose levels, reducing the risk of type 2 diabetes, which is a major cardiovascular risk factor.
  • Body weight. Exercise supports healthy weight management, which reduces the mechanical and metabolic load on the heart.
  • Stress and inflammation. Regular movement lowers cortisol levels and reduces chronic systemic inflammation, both of which contribute to cardiovascular disease.

How Much Exercise Do You Need?

The American Heart Association and the U.S. Department of Health and Human Services recommend that adults get at least 150 minutes per week of moderate-intensity aerobic activity, or 75 minutes per week of vigorous-intensity activity, plus muscle-strengthening activities on two or more days per week. Moderate intensity means you can talk but not sing during the activity—think brisk walking, cycling at a conversational pace, or swimming laps at a comfortable speed.

For people living in Central Oregon, the options for heart-healthy exercise are abundant. Walking or jogging on the Deschutes River Trail, cycling the road or mountain bike trails, cross-country skiing at Virginia Meissner, or hiking to Tumalo Falls all count toward your weekly total. The key is consistency rather than intensity. A daily 30-minute walk provides more cardiovascular benefit than an occasional weekend marathon effort followed by days of inactivity.

Breaking It Down

If 150 minutes per week feels overwhelming, the good news is that exercise can be accumulated in shorter bouts throughout the day. Research shows that three 10-minute walks provide similar cardiovascular benefits to one continuous 30-minute walk. You can also build activity into your routine by:

  • Taking the stairs instead of the elevator
  • Walking or cycling for short errands instead of driving
  • Standing and moving every 30 to 60 minutes if you have a desk job
  • Adding a post-meal walk to your daily schedule (even 10 minutes after dinner helps regulate blood sugar)
  • Joining a walking group or fitness class for social motivation and accountability

Strength Training and Heart Health

While aerobic exercise gets most of the attention when it comes to heart health, strength training plays an important supporting role. Resistance exercise improves metabolic health by increasing muscle mass, which helps the body use glucose more efficiently. It also supports bone density, joint stability, and functional independence—all of which contribute to the ability to stay physically active long-term.

For heart health, the American Heart Association recommends moderate-to-high intensity muscle-strengthening activity on at least two days per week. This can include bodyweight exercises (squats, lunges, push-ups, planks), resistance band work, free weights, or machines. Focus on major muscle groups and aim for two to three sets of 8 to 12 repetitions per exercise.

If you are new to strength training or have an existing heart condition, working with a physical therapist can help you learn proper form, choose appropriate resistance levels, and avoid exercises that may be contraindicated for your specific situation.

How Physical Therapy Supports Heart Health

Physical therapists are movement specialists trained to help people of all fitness levels exercise safely and effectively. For cardiovascular health specifically, a PT can help in several ways:

  • Post-cardiac event rehabilitation. After a heart attack, heart surgery, or procedure like stenting, cardiac rehabilitation programs guided by physical therapists help patients safely return to activity and reduce the risk of future events.
  • Managing exercise with chronic conditions. If you have diabetes, hypertension, arthritis, or other conditions that complicate exercise, a PT can design a program that accounts for your limitations while still providing cardiovascular benefit.
  • Overcoming pain barriers. Musculoskeletal pain is one of the most common reasons people stop exercising. Physical therapy can address the underlying cause of pain so you can maintain an active lifestyle.
  • Building exercise habits. A structured program with regular check-ins provides accountability and progressive challenge, which are key factors in maintaining long-term exercise adherence.

Simple Steps to Start Today

You do not need a gym membership, expensive equipment, or a block of free time to start improving your heart health. Here are five practical actions you can take this week:

  1. Walk for 10 minutes after one meal each day. This is one of the simplest and most effective interventions for blood sugar regulation and cardiovascular health.
  2. Set a movement alarm. If you work at a desk, set a timer to stand up and move for two minutes every hour. Even brief movement breaks reduce the cardiovascular risk associated with prolonged sitting.
  3. Add two bodyweight exercises to your morning routine. Squats and push-ups (modified if needed) take less than five minutes and provide meaningful strength benefits.
  4. Monitor your resting heart rate. A lower resting heart rate generally indicates better cardiovascular fitness. Track it each morning to see how it changes over time as you become more active.
  5. Schedule a check-up. Know your blood pressure, cholesterol, and blood sugar numbers. Understanding your baseline helps you set meaningful goals and track progress.

When to Seek Professional Guidance

While most people can safely begin a walking program without medical clearance, you should consult a healthcare provider before starting a new exercise program if you have a known heart condition, experience chest pain or shortness of breath with exertion, have uncontrolled high blood pressure, or have been sedentary for an extended period.
