Holiday Gift Guide: Home Exercise Equipment

PT-approved picks for active people at every level

  1. Home
  2. Blog

Medically reviewed by Jenny McAteer, DPT Published December 16, 2020 · Last reviewed March 2026
December 16, 2020 · 9 min read

Whether you are shopping for a family member who loves to stay active, a friend recovering from an injury, or yourself, home exercise equipment makes a practical and lasting gift. The challenge is knowing which products are genuinely useful and which will end up gathering dust in a closet. As physical therapists, we use and recommend specific equipment every day in our practice, so we have a strong sense of what actually gets used and delivers results.

This gift guide features our team's top recommendations from Resolve Physical Therapy in Bend, Oregon. Every item on this list is something we use with patients in the clinic and prescribe for home exercise programs. They are also affordable, portable, and versatile enough to be used by people of all ages and fitness levels.

Foam Roller

A foam roller is one of the most versatile pieces of exercise equipment you can own. At its most basic, it is a self-massage tool that helps release tight muscles and fascia through a technique called self-myofascial release. But a foam roller can also be used as a balance training tool, a core stability aid, and a thoracic spine mobilization device.

We recommend a medium-density, 36-inch round foam roller for general use. Firm enough to provide meaningful pressure, but not so hard that it is painful. Shorter rollers (18 inches) are more portable and work well for travel. Textured rollers can provide more targeted pressure for experienced users, but a smooth roller is ideal for beginners.

How to Use It

  • Quadriceps: Lie face down with the roller under your thighs. Roll slowly from hip to just above the knee, pausing on tender spots for 20 to 30 seconds.
  • IT band and outer thigh: Lie on your side with the roller under your outer thigh. Roll from hip to knee, supporting yourself with your arms.
  • Thoracic spine: Lie face up with the roller across your mid-back. Support your head with your hands and gently extend over the roller to improve upper back mobility.
  • Calves: Sit with the roller under your calves. Roll from ankle to knee, rotating your leg to address the inner and outer calf.

Resistance Bands (Loop and Long)

Resistance bands are the Swiss Army knife of exercise equipment. A set of loop bands (sometimes called mini bands) and a set of long resistance bands together cost less than a single pair of dumbbells but provide almost limitless exercise options. Bands offer variable resistance—the more you stretch them, the harder the exercise becomes—which is gentler on joints than free weights and ideal for rehabilitation and prehabilitation.

We recommend purchasing a set of at least three resistance levels (light, medium, heavy) in both loop and long band styles. Loop bands are excellent for hip strengthening exercises like clamshells, lateral band walks, and monster walks. Long bands are ideal for rows, chest presses, shoulder external rotation, and assisted stretching.

Starter Exercises

  • Lateral band walks: Place a loop band around your ankles or just above your knees. Step sideways with control for 10 to 15 steps in each direction. This exercise strengthens the hip abductors, which are essential for knee stability and running form.
  • Band pull-aparts: Hold a long band at shoulder height with both hands. Pull the band apart by squeezing your shoulder blades together. This exercise counteracts the forward-shoulder posture caused by desk work and phone use.
  • Banded squats: Place a loop band just above your knees and perform squats while actively pressing your knees outward against the band. This reinforces proper squat mechanics and activates the glutes.

Balance Pad

A balance pad is a simple piece of dense foam that creates an unstable surface for standing exercises. Standing on a balance pad challenges the proprioceptive system—the body's sense of position in space—and strengthens the small stabilizing muscles in the ankles, knees, and hips. This makes it an excellent tool for injury prevention, post-injury rehabilitation, and fall prevention in older adults.

Balance pads are particularly useful for runners, skiers, hikers, and anyone returning to activity after an ankle sprain or knee injury. They are also one of the most effective tools for improving balance in older adults, which is a critical factor in reducing fall risk.

Progressive Balance Exercises

  1. Two-leg stance: Stand on the pad with both feet for 30 to 60 seconds. When this becomes easy, close your eyes.
  2. Single-leg stance: Stand on one foot for 30 seconds. Progress to eyes closed.
  3. Single-leg squat: Perform a shallow squat on one leg while standing on the pad.
  4. Reach and tap: Stand on one leg on the pad and reach your free foot forward, to the side, and behind you, tapping the floor lightly.

Lacrosse Ball or Massage Ball

A lacrosse ball provides more targeted pressure than a foam roller and is ideal for releasing tight spots in the glutes, plantar fascia, upper back, and shoulders. It is small enough to fit in a gym bag, desk drawer, or travel bag, making it one of the most portable recovery tools available.

To use a lacrosse ball for the plantar fascia, stand with the ball under your foot and roll it slowly from heel to toe with moderate pressure. For the glutes or upper back, place the ball between your body and a wall and lean into it, moving slowly until you find a tender point. Hold for 30 seconds or until you feel the tension release.

Aerobic Step Platform

An adjustable aerobic step is more versatile than it might appear at first glance. Beyond step aerobics, it can be used for Bulgarian split squats, step-ups, calf raises, incline and decline push-ups, triceps dips, and hip flexor stretches. Having an elevated surface at home opens up dozens of exercise variations that would otherwise require gym equipment.

Look for a step with adjustable risers that allow you to change the height. A lower step is better for rehabilitation and beginners, while a higher step provides a greater challenge for strength and power exercises.

Yoga Mat

A quality yoga mat defines your exercise space and makes floor exercises more comfortable. For physical therapy purposes, a mat that is at least 6mm thick provides adequate cushioning for exercises performed on your back, stomach, or knees. Look for a mat with a non-slip surface to prevent sliding during planks, push-ups, and balance work.

Beyond exercise, a yoga mat is useful for daily stretching routines, meditation, and even as a comfortable surface for reading or working on the floor. It is one of those items that seems simple but gets used every day once you have one.

TheraBand FlexBar

The TheraBand FlexBar is a specialized resistance device designed for treating and preventing elbow tendon injuries, including tennis elbow (lateral epicondylitis) and golfer's elbow (medial epicondylitis). The eccentric exercise protocol performed with the FlexBar, known as the Tyler Twist, has strong research support for reducing pain and improving grip strength in people with lateral epicondylitis.

FlexBars come in several resistance levels. Start with the lightest resistance (yellow or red) and progress to heavier bars as your strength improves. This is an excellent gift for climbers, tennis players, golfers, weightlifters, or anyone who works extensively with their hands.

Building Your Home Gym

You do not need a large budget or a dedicated room to build an effective home exercise setup. With a foam roller, a set of resistance bands, a balance pad, a lacrosse ball, and a yoga mat, you have everything you need to perform a comprehensive stretching, strengthening, and recovery program. The total investment is typically less than the cost of a single month at most gyms, and the equipment will last for years.
