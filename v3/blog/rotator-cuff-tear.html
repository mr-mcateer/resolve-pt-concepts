Do I Have a Rotator Cuff Tear?

5 signs to watch for and what to do about them

  1. Home
  2. Blog

Written by Jenny McAteer, DPT Published July 28, 2020 · Last reviewed March 2026
July 28, 2020 · 9 min read

Shoulder pain is one of the most common reasons people visit a physical therapist, and the rotator cuff is frequently the source. Whether you felt a sudden pop during a workout or have noticed a gradual increase in shoulder discomfort over weeks and months, understanding the signs of a rotator cuff tear can help you seek the right treatment at the right time—and potentially avoid surgery.

What Is the Rotator Cuff?

The rotator cuff is a group of four muscles and their associated tendons that wrap around the head of the humerus (upper arm bone) and hold it securely within the shallow socket of the shoulder blade. These four muscles are the supraspinatus, infraspinatus, teres minor, and subscapularis. Together, they perform two critical functions: they stabilize the ball of the shoulder in the socket during movement, and they produce the rotational forces needed for reaching, lifting, throwing, and pulling.

Unlike the hip joint, where a deep bony socket provides inherent stability, the shoulder joint sacrifices stability for range of motion. The shoulder can move in almost every direction, which makes it incredibly versatile but also inherently vulnerable. The rotator cuff compensates for this lack of bony stability, which is why damage to these muscles or tendons has such a significant impact on shoulder function.

Rotator cuff tears can be partial (only part of the tendon is damaged) or complete (the tendon is fully detached from the bone). They can result from a single traumatic event, such as a fall or heavy lift, or from gradual degeneration over time due to repetitive overhead activity, aging, or reduced blood supply to the tendons.

5 Signs You May Have a Rotator Cuff Tear

1. Pain at Rest and at Night

One of the hallmarks of a rotator cuff tear is pain that persists even when you are not using your shoulder. Many patients report a deep, dull ache in the shoulder that worsens at night, particularly when lying on the affected side. This nighttime pain is often the symptom that finally prompts people to seek medical attention, because it disrupts sleep and cannot be attributed to overuse during the day.

The reason the rotator cuff hurts at rest is related to inflammation and swelling within the subacromial space. When you lie down, the position of the shoulder can increase pressure on the irritated tendons, intensifying the pain. If your shoulder pain consistently wakes you up or prevents you from falling asleep, it is worth having it evaluated.

2. Difficulty with Overhead Activities

The rotator cuff muscles are most active during overhead movements, so difficulty reaching above your head is a telling sign. You may notice that lifting objects off high shelves, washing your hair, or reaching for a seatbelt has become painful or feels weaker than it used to. Some patients describe a specific arc of motion—typically between 60 and 120 degrees of elevation—where the pain is most intense. This is known as a "painful arc" and is characteristic of subacromial impingement or a supraspinatus tear.

3. Pain in the Upper Arm

Rotator cuff pain is often felt not in the shoulder itself but in the lateral (outer) aspect of the upper arm, roughly where a military patch would sit. This referred pain pattern is common because the nerves serving the rotator cuff also supply sensation to the deltoid region. Patients frequently come in pointing to their upper arm and saying they thought they had a biceps problem, only to learn that the pain is originating from the rotator cuff.

If you have pain in the upper arm that worsens when you lift your arm to the side or rotate it outward, the rotator cuff should be considered as a potential source, even if the shoulder joint itself does not hurt.

4. Limited Ability to Reach Behind Your Back

Reaching behind your back—to fasten a bra, tuck in a shirt, or reach into a back pocket—requires a combination of internal rotation and extension at the shoulder. When the subscapularis (the rotator cuff muscle on the front of the shoulder blade) is damaged, or when inflammation limits the available space in the joint, these movements become painful and restricted.

Try this simple test: reach one hand behind your back and try to touch the bottom of your opposite shoulder blade. If you cannot reach as high as the other arm, or if the movement produces significant pain, you may have rotator cuff involvement.

5. Grinding, Cracking, or Clicking Sensations

A grinding, cracking, or clicking sensation during shoulder movement—called crepitus—can indicate changes in the way the shoulder joint surfaces are interacting. In the context of a rotator cuff tear, crepitus may occur because the damaged tendon allows the humeral head to migrate upward, reducing the space between the ball and the acromion. This can cause the tendon to catch or rub against the bony surface during movement.

Not all clicking or popping in the shoulder indicates a tear. Many people have painless clicking that is completely normal. However, clicking accompanied by pain, weakness, or a catching sensation warrants further evaluation.

The Good News: Most Rotator Cuff Injuries Improve Without Surgery

Research consistently shows that approximately 80% of rotator cuff tears can be managed successfully with nonsurgical treatment. Physical therapy is the cornerstone of conservative management, and multiple studies have demonstrated that a structured rehabilitation program produces outcomes comparable to surgery for many partial tears and some full-thickness tears, particularly in patients over 60.

Physical therapy for a rotator cuff tear typically includes:

  • Pain management: Manual therapy, ice, activity modification, and positioning strategies to reduce inflammation and manage symptoms in the early phase.
  • Range of motion restoration: Gentle passive and active-assisted exercises to maintain or improve shoulder mobility without stressing the healing tendon.
  • Rotator cuff strengthening: Progressive resistance exercises for the rotator cuff muscles, beginning with isometric (no-motion) contractions and advancing to isotonic exercises with bands or light weights.
  • Scapular stabilization: Exercises targeting the muscles that control the shoulder blade, which provides the foundation for rotator cuff function.
  • Functional training: Sport-specific or activity-specific exercises that prepare the shoulder for the demands of your daily life, work, or recreational activities.

Surgery may be recommended for traumatic full-thickness tears in younger, active patients, tears that do not respond to three to six months of physical therapy, or tears associated with significant weakness that limits daily function. Even after rotator cuff surgery, physical therapy is essential for restoring range of motion, rebuilding strength, and returning to normal activities.

When to See a Physical Therapist

You do not need to wait for a definitive diagnosis to begin physical therapy. In fact, early intervention typically leads to faster recovery and better outcomes. Oregon is a direct-access state, meaning you can see a physical therapist without a referral from a physician. If your shoulder pain has persisted for more than a week, is waking you at night, or is limiting your ability to perform daily activities, schedule an evaluation.
