What Is Vestibular Rehabilitation Therapy?

Vestibular rehabilitation therapy (VRT) is a specialized, exercise-based program designed to treat dizziness, vertigo, balance problems, and other symptoms caused by dysfunction in the vestibular system. The vestibular system, housed within the inner ear, is responsible for detecting head movement and position relative to gravity. It works in concert with your visual system and proprioceptive sensors throughout your body to maintain equilibrium, spatial orientation, and clear vision during movement.

When the vestibular system is disrupted by injury, infection, degeneration, or other causes, the brain receives conflicting signals about body position and motion. This mismatch produces the symptoms that bring patients to our clinic: spinning sensations, lightheadedness, unsteadiness, difficulty walking, visual disturbances, and a pervasive sense of imbalance that can be profoundly disabling.

Vestibular rehabilitation is delivered by specialty-trained physical therapists who have completed advanced education in the assessment and treatment of vestibular disorders. At Resolve Physical Therapy in Bend, Oregon, Jenny McAteer, DPT, brings focused expertise in vestibular evaluation and treatment to every patient encounter. Each session is conducted one-on-one, allowing for the precise observation and real-time adjustment that vestibular conditions demand.

VRT is not a one-size-fits-all approach. The exercises and techniques used are selected based on your specific diagnosis, the severity of your symptoms, and how your vestibular system responds to testing. Treatment may include canalith repositioning maneuvers for BPPV, habituation exercises to reduce motion sensitivity, gaze stabilization drills to improve visual clarity during head movement, and balance retraining to restore confidence in standing and walking. The goal is always to improve your functional abilities, reduce fall risk, and enhance your quality of life so you can fully participate in the active lifestyle that Bend, Oregon offers.

How Vestibular Rehabilitation Works

Effective vestibular rehabilitation begins with a thorough assessment. At your initial evaluation at Resolve Physical Therapy, your therapist will conduct a comprehensive examination that includes several specialized components designed to identify the specific nature and source of your vestibular dysfunction.

The Vestibular Evaluation

Your therapist begins by reviewing your symptom history in detail: when the dizziness or imbalance started, what triggers it, how long episodes last, and what makes them better or worse. A detailed medical history review identifies any medications, prior injuries, surgeries, or medical conditions that may contribute to your symptoms.

The physical examination includes oculomotor screening, which evaluates how your eyes track moving targets and respond to rapid head movements. Abnormal eye movements, called nystagmus, provide critical diagnostic clues about which part of the vestibular system is affected. Positional testing, including the Dix-Hallpike and Roll tests, determines whether BPPV is present and identifies which semicircular canal is involved.

Balance and gait assessment measures your ability to maintain standing balance under progressively challenging conditions: eyes open versus closed, firm surface versus foam surface, and with head movements. Gait analysis evaluates your walking pattern, stride symmetry, ability to turn, and steadiness on varied surfaces. Cervical spine screening examines whether neck stiffness or cervicogenic dizziness is contributing to your symptoms.

Treatment Principles

Based on the findings from your evaluation, your therapist selects from three primary categories of vestibular exercises, each targeting a different mechanism of recovery.

Habituation exercises are used when dizziness is triggered by visual stimuli or specific head movements. These exercises involve repeated exposure to the provoking motion or visual environment in a controlled setting. Over time, the brain learns to suppress the abnormal vestibular signals, and the dizziness response diminishes. Habituation is particularly effective for patients who feel dizzy when turning their head quickly, looking at busy visual environments, or changing positions.

Gaze stabilization exercises train the vestibulo-ocular reflex (VOR), the mechanism that keeps your vision clear during head movement. When the VOR is impaired, the world appears to bounce or blur when you move your head, a symptom called oscillopsia. Gaze stabilization drills involve focusing on a visual target while moving the head at progressively faster speeds. These exercises drive neuroplastic adaptation in the brain, enhancing its ability to compensate for reduced vestibular input.

Balance retraining exercises challenge the three sensory systems that contribute to balance: vision, vestibular input, and proprioception (the sense of body position from receptors in muscles, tendons, and joints). By systematically reducing reliance on one system at a time (for example, standing with eyes closed to remove visual input), these exercises force the brain to better integrate the available information, resulting in improved stability and reduced fall risk.

The overarching goals of vestibular rehabilitation are to improve your functional abilities so you can perform daily tasks safely and independently, to reduce your risk of falling, and to enhance your overall quality of life. For many patients in Bend, this means returning to hiking on uneven terrain, cycling on the region's trails, or simply walking through a crowded farmers market without feeling unsteady.

Vertigo and BPPV

Vertigo is the false sensation that you or your surroundings are spinning, rotating, or tilting. It is not merely feeling lightheaded or faint. True vertigo involves a perception of movement where none exists, and it originates from a dysfunction in the vestibular system rather than from cardiovascular or metabolic causes. Vertigo episodes can last seconds to hours depending on the underlying condition, and they are often accompanied by nausea, difficulty focusing, and unsteadiness.

Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (BPPV)

The most common cause of vertigo is benign paroxysmal positional vertigo, known as BPPV. This condition accounts for approximately 50 percent of all peripheral vestibular disorders and affects an estimated 2.4 percent of people at some point in their lives. Despite its prevalence, BPPV is frequently misdiagnosed or undertreated, leaving many patients suffering unnecessarily for weeks or months.

BPPV occurs when tiny calcium carbonate crystals called otoconia become dislodged from the utricle (a gravity-sensing organ in the inner ear) and migrate into one of the three semicircular canals. These displaced crystals create abnormal fluid movement within the canal when the head changes position, sending false signals to the brain about head rotation. The result is brief but intense episodes of spinning vertigo triggered by specific head movements, such as rolling over in bed, looking up, or bending down.

The hallmark of BPPV is that vertigo episodes are brief, typically lasting less than 60 seconds, and are provoked by specific changes in head position. Patients often describe the spinning as starting a few seconds after they move their head and then gradually subsiding. Between episodes, patients may feel normal or may have a residual sense of unsteadiness.

The Epley Maneuver and Canalith Repositioning

The primary treatment for BPPV is canalith repositioning, a technique that uses a specific sequence of head and body positions to guide the displaced crystals out of the affected semicircular canal and back into the utricle, where they can be reabsorbed. The most well-known of these techniques is the Epley maneuver, designed for the posterior canal, which is the most commonly affected.

During the Epley maneuver, your therapist moves you through a precise sequence of positions while observing your eye movements to confirm that the crystals are moving in the expected direction. The entire procedure takes approximately five to ten minutes and is performed on the treatment table in the clinic. Many patients experience significant or complete resolution of vertigo after a single treatment session.

For BPPV affecting other canals, different repositioning maneuvers are used. The Lempert (barbecue roll) maneuver treats horizontal canal BPPV, while the deep head-hanging maneuver addresses anterior canal involvement. At Resolve Physical Therapy, our positional testing identifies which specific canal is involved, ensuring the correct maneuver is applied.

Treatment success rates for BPPV are exceptionally high. Studies consistently show that canalith repositioning resolves BPPV in approximately 80 to 90 percent of patients within one to three treatment sessions. Some patients do experience recurrence, particularly within the first year, but repeat treatment is equally effective. Your therapist will also teach you self-treatment techniques you can use at home if symptoms return.

Dizziness: Assessment and Treatment

Dizziness is a broad term that encompasses several distinct sensations, and understanding exactly what a patient means by "dizzy" is essential for accurate diagnosis and effective treatment. Dizziness differs from vertigo in that it does not necessarily involve a spinning sensation. Patients describe dizziness in many ways: feeling lightheaded, woozy, foggy, off-balance, floating, rocking, or simply "not right."

The causes of dizziness are varied. Vestibular neuritis, an inflammation of the vestibular nerve typically triggered by a viral infection, causes sudden severe dizziness and imbalance that gradually improves over days to weeks. Vestibular migraines produce episodes of dizziness and motion sensitivity associated with migraine headaches, and they affect a significant percentage of migraine sufferers. Persistent postural-perceptual dizziness (PPPD) is a chronic condition in which patients feel unsteady and sensitive to visual stimuli on most days. Age-related vestibular decline causes gradual loss of balance function that increases fall risk. Medication side effects, cardiovascular conditions, and anxiety disorders can also produce dizziness.

At Resolve Physical Therapy, the vestibular evaluation process systematically differentiates among these causes. Your therapist examines eye movements, tests positional responses, assesses balance systems, and reviews your medical history to identify the specific mechanism driving your symptoms. This diagnostic precision allows treatment to be targeted and effective rather than generic.

Habituation-Based Treatment

For patients whose dizziness is provoked by specific movements or visual environments, habituation exercises form the core of treatment. The principle behind habituation is that the brain can learn to suppress an abnormal dizziness response through repeated, controlled exposure to the provoking stimulus.

Your therapist identifies which specific movements and positions trigger your dizziness, then designs a program of exercises that reproduce those triggers at a manageable intensity. As you perform these exercises consistently over days and weeks, the brain's dizziness response gradually diminishes. The exercises are progressed systematically, increasing speed, complexity, and duration as your tolerance improves.

Habituation is particularly effective for patients with vestibular neuritis, vestibular migraines, and PPPD. Many patients notice meaningful improvement within two to four weeks of consistent exercise, with significant resolution of symptoms occurring over six to twelve weeks of dedicated rehabilitation.

Post-Concussion Vestibular Rehabilitation

Concussions frequently affect the vestibular system, and vestibular symptoms are among the strongest predictors of prolonged recovery after a head injury. Research indicates that up to 60 percent of concussion patients experience dizziness, and approximately 30 percent develop balance problems that persist beyond the initial acute phase. In Central Oregon, concussions from skiing, snowboarding, mountain biking, and contact sports are common presentations at our Bend clinic.

Vestibular symptoms after concussion can include dizziness triggered by head movement, difficulty maintaining balance, visual motion sensitivity (discomfort in visually busy environments), trouble reading or using screens, headaches provoked by visual or vestibular stimulation, and a sense of fogginess or disorientation in crowded spaces. These symptoms frequently overlap with cognitive and emotional effects of concussion, creating a complex clinical picture that requires expert evaluation.

At Resolve Physical Therapy, post-concussion vestibular rehabilitation begins with a comprehensive assessment that differentiates among the various mechanisms that may be causing your symptoms. Central vestibular processing disruption, peripheral vestibular injury, cervicogenic dizziness from neck involvement, and oculomotor dysfunction each require different treatment approaches, and many concussion patients present with more than one contributing factor.

Gradual Return to Activity

A cornerstone of modern concussion management is the graduated return-to-activity protocol. This structured, stepwise approach ensures that patients increase their physical and cognitive demands at a pace that their healing brain can tolerate. Your physical therapist monitors your symptoms at each stage and adjusts the progression accordingly.

The protocol typically begins with light aerobic activity below the symptom threshold, such as walking or stationary cycling at a comfortable pace. If tolerated, activity is progressed to sport-specific exercise, non-contact training drills, full-contact practice (where applicable), and finally return to full participation. Each stage is maintained for at least 24 hours without symptom exacerbation before advancing.

Visual-Vestibular Integration Exercises

Many concussion patients experience difficulty with visual-vestibular integration, the process by which the brain coordinates information from the eyes and vestibular system to maintain clear vision and stable posture during movement. When this integration is disrupted, patients struggle with activities that require simultaneous visual tracking and head movement, such as scanning a grocery store aisle, following a ball during sports, or driving.

Visual-vestibular integration exercises at Resolve PT progressively challenge the brain to coordinate eye and head movements. These exercises might include tracking a moving target while walking, reading text during head turns, or navigating obstacles while maintaining visual focus on a distant target. Smooth pursuit training, saccadic eye movement drills, and convergence exercises address oculomotor dysfunction that commonly accompanies vestibular concussion symptoms.

The one-on-one treatment model at Resolve Physical Therapy is particularly valuable for concussion patients because symptom responses are highly individual and can change rapidly during a treatment session. Your therapist continuously monitors your symptoms and adjusts exercise intensity in real time, ensuring you are working at the optimal level to drive recovery without exacerbating symptoms.

Balance Impairments and Fall Prevention

Balance is a complex skill that relies on the coordinated input of three sensory systems: the vestibular system (inner ear), the visual system (eyes), and the somatosensory system (proprioceptors in muscles, tendons, and joints). The brain integrates information from all three systems to generate appropriate motor responses that keep you upright and stable. When any of these systems is compromised, or when the brain's ability to integrate their signals diminishes, balance impairment results.

Age-related balance decline is a gradual process driven by natural changes in all three sensory systems. The vestibular organs lose hair cells over time, reducing the sensitivity and accuracy of motion detection. Visual acuity and depth perception decrease. Proprioceptive sensitivity in the feet and ankles diminishes, particularly in the presence of conditions such as peripheral neuropathy. The central nervous system's processing speed also slows, increasing reaction times when balance is challenged.

These changes are not inevitable sentences to inactivity or falls. Research consistently demonstrates that targeted balance training can significantly improve stability and reduce fall risk in older adults, even those with multiple contributing factors. The brain's remarkable capacity for neuroplastic adaptation means that balance can be trained and improved at any age.

Fall Prevention at Resolve PT

At Resolve Physical Therapy, our balance and fall prevention program begins with standardized balance assessments that measure your standing stability, dynamic balance, gait characteristics, and fall risk. These objective measures provide a baseline against which we track your progress and establish clear, achievable goals.

Your balance training program is individually designed based on which sensory systems are contributing to your impairment. Exercises may include standing on foam surfaces to challenge proprioception, performing head turns during walking to engage the vestibular system, navigating obstacle courses to improve dynamic balance, and practicing reactive stepping to improve your response to unexpected perturbations.

Strength training is an essential component of fall prevention, as muscle weakness, particularly in the hips, knees, and ankles, is one of the most modifiable risk factors for falls. Your program will include progressive resistance exercises targeting the lower extremity muscles that are critical for maintaining upright posture, recovering from trips and stumbles, and navigating the uneven terrain that characterizes much of Bend's outdoor landscape.

Proprioception Training

Proprioception, your body's awareness of its position in space, plays a vital role in maintaining balance during everyday activities. Proprioceptive input from sensors in your joints, muscles, and tendons tells your brain where your limbs are and how fast they are moving, without requiring you to look at them. This sense is essential for walking on uneven ground, climbing stairs, and recovering from stumbles.

Proprioceptive training at Resolve PT involves exercises that progressively challenge your body's position sense. Single-leg standing, tandem walking (heel-to-toe), standing on unstable surfaces, reaching exercises that shift your center of gravity, and eyes-closed balance drills all stimulate proprioceptive adaptation. As your proprioceptive acuity improves, you gain confidence in situations that previously felt uncertain or threatening.

For patients in Central Oregon, improved balance and proprioception translate directly to the ability to hike forest trails, walk on snow and ice, navigate the varied terrain of farmers markets and outdoor festivals, and participate fully in the active community that makes this region special.

Frequently Asked Questions

Vertigo has several possible causes. The most common is benign paroxysmal positional vertigo (BPPV), which occurs when tiny calcium carbonate crystals in the inner ear become dislodged and migrate into the semicircular canals. Other causes include vestibular neuritis (inflammation of the vestibular nerve), Meniere's disease, vestibular migraines, head injuries and concussions, and certain medications. A thorough evaluation by a vestibular-trained physical therapist can identify the specific cause and guide appropriate treatment.

The number of sessions varies depending on the condition being treated. BPPV often resolves in one to three treatment sessions with canalith repositioning maneuvers. Vestibular neuritis and post-concussion dizziness typically require six to twelve sessions over several weeks. Chronic balance disorders may benefit from ongoing therapy. At Resolve Physical Therapy in Bend, your treatment plan is reassessed regularly to ensure you are making progress and your program is adjusted as needed.

Many forms of dizziness respond very well to physical therapy. BPPV is highly treatable with canalith repositioning maneuvers, with success rates above 90 percent. For other vestibular conditions, physical therapy can significantly reduce symptoms and improve function through habituation exercises, gaze stabilization training, and balance retraining. While some conditions are managed rather than cured outright, most patients experience substantial improvement in symptoms and functional ability with vestibular rehabilitation.
