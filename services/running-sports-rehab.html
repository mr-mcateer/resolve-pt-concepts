The $300 Comprehensive Running Evaluation

Our running evaluation is a two-appointment, in-depth assessment designed to identify the root causes of running pain, inefficiency, or recurrent injury. At $300 for both sessions, this program provides a level of biomechanical analysis typically available only at university research labs or elite training centers.

What Your Running Evaluation Includes

The evaluation spans two appointments to ensure thorough assessment and time for your therapist to analyze the data collected during your first visit. Here is what each session covers:

Session 1: Assessment and Data Collection

  • Thorough physical therapy evaluation — Your therapist reviews your injury history, training load, goals, and current symptoms. A full musculoskeletal screen assesses joint mobility, muscle strength, flexibility, and functional movement patterns relevant to running.
  • Multi-angle video gait analysis — Using high-definition slow-motion video, your therapist records your running gait from anterior, posterior, and lateral views on our clinic treadmill. This footage is analyzed frame by frame to identify asymmetries, compensatory patterns, and biomechanical faults.
  • DorsaVi wearable sensor analysis — Two lightweight sensors are placed on your shins while you run on the treadmill. These FDA-cleared devices capture objective data on five critical biomechanical metrics: ground reaction forces, initial peak acceleration, ground contact time, cadence, and single-limb stability.
  • Shoe evaluation — Your therapist examines your current running footwear for wear patterns, support characteristics, and compatibility with your foot type and gait pattern.

Session 2: Analysis, Education, and Treatment Plan

  • Data review and interpretation — Your therapist walks you through the DorsaVi sensor report and video analysis, explaining what the numbers and footage reveal about your running mechanics.
  • Personalized treatment plan — Based on the clinical evaluation and biomechanical data, your therapist designs a targeted program addressing your specific deficits. This may include corrective exercises, strength training, manual therapy, running form cues, and training modifications.
  • Home exercise program — You receive a documented set of exercises to perform between sessions, progressed over time as your movement patterns improve.
  • Return-to-run progression — If you are recovering from injury, your therapist outlines a structured and gradual return-to-running protocol based on your body's response to loading.

DorsaVi Biomechanical Sensor Technology

DorsaVi is a clinically validated wearable sensor system used by professional sports teams, Olympic training centers, and leading rehabilitation clinics worldwide. Resolve Physical Therapy is proud to be one of the few clinics in Central Oregon offering this technology to our patients.

What DorsaVi Sensors Measure

The two small sensors are secured to the front of each shin using medical-grade adhesive. While you run at your natural pace on the treadmill, the sensors capture data at high sampling rates and transmit it wirelessly to the analysis software. The five primary metrics include:

  • Ground reaction forces — The magnitude and pattern of forces transmitted through your lower legs each time your foot strikes the ground. Elevated or asymmetric forces are associated with stress fractures, shin splints, and joint overloading.
  • Initial peak acceleration — How abruptly your tibia decelerates at foot strike. High initial peak acceleration indicates aggressive impact loading, which contributes to bone stress injuries and joint wear.
  • Ground contact time — The duration each foot remains on the ground during the stance phase. Prolonged ground contact time may indicate weakness, fatigue, or inefficient running mechanics.
  • Cadence — Your step rate per minute. Cadence is one of the most modifiable running mechanics variables and directly influences impact loading and injury risk.
  • Single-limb stability — How well each leg controls motion in the frontal and transverse planes during the single-leg stance phase. Poor single-limb stability is a predictor of knee, hip, and ankle injuries in runners.

Why Objective Data Matters

Visual gait analysis is valuable, but the human eye can miss subtle asymmetries and internal loading patterns that sensors detect with precision. DorsaVi data allows your therapist to quantify problems, set measurable treatment goals, and track your progress with before-and-after comparisons. This data-driven approach removes guesswork and ensures your rehabilitation targets the actual mechanical factors driving your injury.

Common Running Injuries We Treat

Research consistently shows that approximately 65% of runners experience at least one injury in any given year. Many of these injuries are overuse conditions driven by biomechanical inefficiencies, training errors, or inadequate recovery. At Resolve Physical Therapy, we treat the full spectrum of running-related injuries with a combination of manual therapy, targeted strengthening, biomechanical correction, and training guidance.

Plantar Fasciitis

Plantar fasciitis is one of the most common causes of heel and arch pain in runners. The plantar fascia, a thick band of connective tissue running along the bottom of the foot, becomes irritated through repetitive loading. Treatment at Resolve PT typically includes manual therapy to the foot and calf complex, progressive loading exercises for the plantar fascia and Achilles tendon, gait modification to reduce forefoot loading, and footwear assessment. Shockwave therapy may also be recommended for stubborn cases.

IT Band Syndrome

Iliotibial band syndrome causes pain on the outside of the knee, typically worsening with downhill running or increased mileage. Despite its name, the IT band itself is rarely the true source of the problem. Our approach focuses on addressing hip weakness, poor pelvic control, and movement patterns that cause excessive compression at the lateral knee.

Patellofemoral Pain Syndrome

Often called runner's knee, patellofemoral pain manifests as pain around or behind the kneecap. This condition responds well to a combination of quadriceps strengthening with emphasis on the vastus medialis oblique, hip abductor and external rotator strengthening, patellar taping or bracing, and gait retraining to reduce knee valgus during foot strike.

Shin Splints (Medial Tibial Stress Syndrome)

Shin splints present as diffuse pain along the inside edge of the tibia, often worsening at the start of a run. Left untreated, this condition can progress to tibial stress fractures. DorsaVi sensor analysis is particularly valuable for shin splint patients because it quantifies the tibial loading forces that must be reduced for recovery. Treatment includes load management, calf and tibialis posterior strengthening, and cadence optimization.

Calf Strains and Achilles Tendinopathy

The calf complex bears tremendous loads during running, and strains of the gastrocnemius or soleus muscles are common in hill runners and speed-work enthusiasts. Achilles tendinopathy, a degenerative condition of the Achilles tendon, requires a specific heavy-load eccentric strengthening protocol. Our therapists guide patients through progressive loading programs tailored to their tendon's capacity and tolerance.

Hip Bursitis and Gluteal Tendinopathy

Lateral hip pain is frequently misdiagnosed as bursitis when the underlying issue is gluteal tendinopathy, a condition affecting the tendons of the gluteus medius and minimus. Treatment focuses on progressive tendon loading, avoiding positions that compress the lateral hip, and addressing contributing factors like pelvic drop during single-leg stance.

Hamstring Strains

Hamstring injuries range from mild strains to complete tears and are common in runners, sprinters, and field sport athletes. Rehabilitation emphasizes progressive eccentric loading, trunk control, and gradual return to high-speed running with specific benchmarks for clearance.

Tibialis Posterior Tendonitis

The tibialis posterior muscle supports the arch of the foot during the stance phase of running. Tendonitis of this muscle causes pain along the inner ankle and arch and may contribute to progressive flat foot deformity. Early treatment with supportive footwear guidance, arch strengthening, and gait modification yields the best outcomes.

Sports Injury Recovery for All Athletes

While our running rehabilitation program is our signature offering, Resolve Physical Therapy treats athletes across all sports and activity levels. Bend's active community includes competitive and recreational skiers, climbers, cyclists, swimmers, golfers, tennis players, and team sport athletes who all benefit from our evidence-based sports rehabilitation approach.

Conditions We Treat in Athletes

  • ACL tears and reconstruction recovery — Structured rehabilitation protocols from post-operative week one through return-to-sport clearance testing, including agility progressions and sport-specific training.
  • Concussion rehabilitation — Graded return-to-activity programs addressing vestibular dysfunction, exertional intolerance, and cervicogenic symptoms following concussion.
  • Hip flexor strains — Common in cyclists, runners, and field sport athletes. Treatment addresses both the acute strain and the underlying movement patterns contributing to recurrence.
  • Shoulder injuries — Rotator cuff strains, labral tears, shoulder instability, and impingement in overhead athletes and climbers.
  • Tennis and golfer's elbow — Lateral and medial epicondylalgia treated with progressive tendon loading, grip strengthening, and activity modification.
  • Groin strains — Adductor strains and sports hernia management with return-to-sport criteria and progressive loading protocols.

Return-to-Sport Protocols

Returning to sport after injury is not simply a matter of pain resolution. At Resolve PT, we use objective functional testing to determine readiness for sport. This may include single-leg hop testing, strength symmetry assessment, dynamic balance evaluation, and sport-specific movement analysis. Our goal is not just to get you back to your sport but to return you stronger and more resilient than before your injury.
